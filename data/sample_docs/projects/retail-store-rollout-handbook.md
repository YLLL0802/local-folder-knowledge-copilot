# Retail Store Rollout Handbook

Document owner: Retail Transformation PMO  
Audience: Project managers, store operations, technology deployment leads  
Classification: Internal  
Effective date: 2026-02-10

## 1. Purpose

This handbook defines the standard approach for opening, remodeling, or technology-enabling retail stores. It is intended for store rollouts that require coordination across real estate, construction, merchandising, IT, HR, finance, supply chain, store operations, and external vendors.

## 2. Rollout Phases

Each rollout follows five phases: feasibility, definition, build, readiness, and stabilization. The project manager may tailor the activities for low-risk remodels, but the entry and exit criteria must be documented.

Feasibility confirms the business case, site constraints, estimated investment, expected sales uplift, technology scope, staffing assumptions, and target launch window. Definition turns the approved concept into a delivery plan, vendor plan, fixture plan, technology design, training approach, and risk register.

Build covers construction, network installation, device staging, point-of-sale configuration, inventory planning, signage, and hiring. Readiness verifies that the store can operate safely and that staff, systems, stock, cash handling, support channels, and escalation paths are ready. Stabilization monitors the first 30 days after opening.

## 3. Governance

The rollout sponsor owns business outcomes and resolves cross-functional priority conflicts. The project manager owns the integrated plan, risk log, dependency tracker, and decision record. Workstream leads own detailed delivery for their domains.

The steering group reviews milestone progress, budget, launch risk, vendor readiness, and customer-impacting decisions. The launch date may not be confirmed publicly until operational readiness and technology readiness are both green or accepted by the sponsor.

## 4. Technology Readiness

Technology readiness includes network connectivity, backup connectivity, endpoint imaging, device inventory, POS certification, payment terminal setup, printer testing, handheld scanner testing, digital signage, Wi-Fi coverage, monitoring, and support handover.

Critical technology defects must have an owner, workaround, severity, and fix date. A store may not open with unresolved defects that prevent sales, refunds, payment acceptance, inventory receiving, employee time capture, or emergency communication.

## 5. Store Operations Readiness

Operations readiness includes staffing plan, training completion, opening procedures, cash handling controls, safe access, incident escalation, visual merchandising, inventory receipt, stockroom organization, customer queue plan, and local compliance checks.

Store managers must complete a dry run before launch. The dry run should simulate opening, receiving goods, customer checkout, refund processing, end-of-day cash reconciliation, incident reporting, and support escalation.

## 6. Vendor and Procurement Controls

Vendor work must be tied to approved statements of work, purchase orders, delivery dates, acceptance criteria, and named business owners. The project manager must track dependencies where one vendor's delay affects another vendor's work.

Invoices must not be approved until goods or services are received and any acceptance criteria are met. Exceptions require sponsor approval and Finance review.

## 7. Stabilization

For 30 days after opening, the project team tracks sales-impacting defects, support tickets, staff feedback, customer complaints, inventory issues, and vendor punch-list items. The project may close when support ownership is accepted and the sponsor confirms that remaining actions can be handled by business-as-usual teams.

## 8. Reference Basis

This internal sample handbook is aligned with public project delivery standards covering staged delivery, readiness gates, operational transition, and controlled closure.
