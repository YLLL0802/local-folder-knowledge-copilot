# Project Delivery Checklist

Document owner: Portfolio Management Office  
Audience: Project managers and project sponsors  
Classification: Internal  
Effective date: 2026-01-30

## 1. Purpose

This checklist defines the minimum evidence required before a project moves from idea to delivery and from delivery to operational handover. It supports consistent governance, risk management, funding decisions, and project closure.

## 2. Intake Gate

Before a project is accepted into the portfolio, the sponsor must provide a short problem statement, expected business outcome, affected teams, estimated cost range, target date, and known constraints. The Portfolio Management Office reviews whether the request is a project, enhancement, operational task, or incident response activity.

The intake decision must identify the sponsor, project manager, preliminary delivery approach, funding source, and required governance path.

## 3. Initiation Gate

The project may begin planning when the sponsor confirms that there is a real business need, the expected benefits are measurable, and the project has a named owner. The project manager must create the project brief, stakeholder map, initial risk log, high-level plan, communication approach, and resource estimate.

Security, Legal, Procurement, Finance, HR, or Privacy review is required when the project changes access controls, uses vendors, processes personal data, affects regulated reporting, changes employee terms, or introduces a new technology.

## 4. Definition Gate

The project may enter delivery only when the following evidence is approved:

- Business case or equivalent funding approval.
- Baseline scope and out-of-scope items.
- Delivery plan with milestones, dependencies, assumptions, and constraints.
- Risk and issue register with owners and mitigation dates.
- Data classification and access model.
- Test approach and acceptance criteria.
- Change control process.
- Support model and draft handover plan.

The approval decision must state whether the project is approved to proceed, approved with conditions, deferred, or stopped.

## 5. Delivery Controls

Project managers must maintain current status, risks, issues, decisions, budget position, and dependency tracking. Material changes to scope, cost, delivery date, business case, vendor commitments, or risk exposure require sponsor approval and may require a formal change request.

Weekly status reports must distinguish completed work from planned work. They must identify blockers, decisions needed, and risks that require escalation. Green status is not allowed when an unresolved blocker threatens the next milestone.

## 6. Readiness Gate

Before go-live, the project must complete user acceptance testing, operational readiness review, security review if applicable, support training, rollback planning, communication to impacted teams, and confirmation that unresolved defects are accepted by the business owner.

The sponsor must confirm that the delivered output still supports the business outcome and that remaining risks are within tolerance.

## 7. Closure Gate

A project may close when outputs are delivered or when the sponsor decides to stop the project. Closure evidence must include benefit ownership, final financial position, outstanding actions, support ownership, retained records, lessons learned, and a decision on whether a post-implementation review is required.

## 8. Reference Basis

This internal sample checklist is aligned with public project delivery guidance that emphasizes governance, stage gates, business justification, assurance, risk management, and controlled closure.
